1.1. General Overview of Text-to-Speech Systems
------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.1.1. Definition and Purpose of Text-to-Speech Technology
-----------------------------------------------------------

Text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis is the automatic conversion of written natural
language text into intelligible, natural-sounding spoken audio. The goal is not
merely to produce any sound corresponding to the input text, but to generate
speech that is simultaneously intelligible — correctly understood by listeners —
and natural — resembling the prosodic and phonetic properties of human speech
(Taylor et al., 1998; Tan et al., 2021).

These two qualities — intelligibility and naturalness — are analytically distinct
and are evaluated by different metrics. Intelligibility is a threshold property:
speech is either understandable or it is not, and it can be measured objectively
through word error rate (WER), which compares a human or ASR transcription of the
synthesized audio to the original text. Naturalness is a continuous perceptual
quality reflecting how closely synthesized speech resembles natural human
production in prosody, rhythm, voice quality, and coarticulation; it is measured
subjectively through the Mean Opinion Score (MOS) protocol defined in ITU-T
Recommendation P.800 (Speech Quality Metrics, 2020–2024). A TTS system can
achieve high intelligibility while scoring low on naturalness — as formant
synthesizers typically do — meaning the two dimensions must be targeted and
evaluated independently.

TTS technology has become a critical component of modern human-computer
interaction across a broad spectrum of applications:

  - Accessibility tools for individuals with visual impairments or reading
    disabilities (screen readers, navigation aids)
  - Voice assistants and conversational agents (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant)
  - Audiobook and podcast generation from written content
  - Navigation and mapping systems providing turn-by-turn guidance
  - Language learning and e-learning platforms with pronunciation models
  - Customer service automation and interactive voice response (IVR) systems
  - Assistive and augmentative communication (AAC) devices for people with
    speech or language disorders
  - Real-time dubbing and localization of video content

Beyond convenience applications, TTS carries direct implications for linguistic
equity and digital inclusion. As a growing share of information, public services,
and social participation migrates to digital platforms, languages that lack
functional speech interfaces are at risk of systematic exclusion from the
benefits of digital transformation (Various authors, 2024). For a speaker of a
low-resource language, the absence of TTS support in navigation systems, screen
readers, or voice interfaces effectively imposes a barrier to equal access to
digital services. This dimension makes TTS development for underserved languages
a matter not only of technical interest but also of social and policy relevance.

Market research indicates that the global TTS market exceeded $3.1 billion in
2023 and is projected to expand at a compound annual growth rate of 14.6%
through 2030 (arXiv:2412.06602, 2024). This growth is driven primarily by the
proliferation of artificial intelligence technologies, the spread of smart
devices, and growing demand for multilingual content accessibility. However,
this market expansion is highly uneven across languages: the overwhelming
majority of commercial investment is directed at a small number of high-resource
languages — English, Mandarin, Spanish, Arabic — while the remaining majority
of the world's approximately 7,000 languages receive little or no commercial
attention (arXiv:2412.06602, 2024).

High-quality TTS technology remains unavailable for the majority of the world's
languages. Azerbaijani — the official language of the Republic of Azerbaijan
and spoken by approximately 35 million people worldwide — is among the
languages underserved by modern speech technology. The existing Azerbaijani TTS
landscape is thin: Samir Rustamov's commercial system (samirrustamov.com)
provides a proprietary service without published technical documentation; the
IS2AI multilingual Turkic TTS system (Yeshpanov et al., 2023) covers Azerbaijani
among ten Turkic languages but is primarily designed for a multilingual setting
rather than a linguistically grounded Azerbaijani-specific architecture; and
Aghalari's (2023) Tacotron 2-based neural TTS was a research prototype trained
on approximately 19 hours of proprietary data, without a published model or
open-source release. No openly available, well-documented system with a
transparent linguistic foundation and published rule base currently exists for
Azerbaijani. Addressing this gap — and producing a documented reference
architecture for Azerbaijani speech synthesis — is the principal motivation of
this dissertation.

1.1.2. Core Components of TTS Systems
--------------------------------------

Despite the diversity of acoustic backends, all TTS systems share a common
two-stage architecture: a linguistic front-end that analyzes and enriches the
input text, and an acoustic back-end that converts the linguistic representation
to a waveform (Tan et al., 2021).

[Figure 1.1: General TTS system architecture — block diagram from text input
to speech output]
[Source: ResearchGate (2021). Text-To-Speech system architecture.
https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Text-To-Speech-system-architecture_fig1_241025907]

The linguistic front-end comprises four principal components:

1. TEXT NORMALIZATION
   Conversion of non-standard words (NSWs) — numerals, abbreviations, symbols,
   dates, times, currency amounts — into their fully expanded, pronounceable
   form. For example, "15.06.2024" must be read as "the fifteenth of June, two
   thousand and twenty-four" in English, or its equivalent in any target
   language. Production systems such as NVIDIA NeMo use weighted finite-state
   transducers (WFSTs) for this process (NVIDIA, 2020–2024). Recent LLM-based
   approaches such as PolyNorm (Apple Machine Learning Research, 2024) achieve
   multilingual normalization from few examples (arXiv:2511.03080).

2. MORPHOLOGICAL ANALYSIS
   Segmentation of words into morphemes, assignment of part-of-speech labels,
   and resolution of lexical ambiguity. Agglutinative languages — Azerbaijani
   among them — present particular challenges at this stage, since a single word
   may consist of a root plus many suffixes, each modifying the pronunciation
   of the whole (MorAz, 2019).

3. GRAPHEME-TO-PHONEME (G2P) CONVERSION
   Mapping of orthographic characters to their phonemic representation, typically
   in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA). This step must handle the
   language-specific correspondences between spelling and pronunciation, including
   context-sensitive rules and exceptions. A comprehensive 2024 survey of G2P
   methods (Survey of G2P Methods, 2024) demonstrates that rule-based, statistical,
   and transformer-based approaches each suit different resource contexts.

4. PROSODY PREDICTION
   Generation of the suprasegmental properties of speech: lexical stress,
   phrase-level pitch accents, intonation contours (F0 trajectories over time),
   phone durations, and pause locations. Recent work on prosody modeling includes
   LSTM-based natural prosody learning (Sensors, 2024) and PitchFlow (Sadekova
   et al., 2024), which adds pitch control to a flow-matching TTS model.

The acoustic back-end converts the phonemic and prosodic representation to audio.
In rule-based systems this involves a formant synthesizer or a concatenative
system that stitches pre-recorded phoneme segments together. In statistical
systems, a parametric model generates acoustic feature sequences, and a vocoder
converts them to a waveform. In neural systems, the acoustic model generates a
mel-spectrogram and a neural vocoder (WaveNet, HiFi-GAN) converts it to high-
fidelity audio (Tan et al., 2021). The back-end is the single largest determinant
of a system's naturalness ceiling: improvements to the linguistic front-end
cannot compensate for limitations in the acoustic model.

TTS system quality is measured along two primary dimensions (Speech Quality
Metrics, 2020–2024):

  - Mean Opinion Score (MOS): human listeners rate naturalness on a five-point
    ITU-T P.800 scale from 1 (bad) to 5 (excellent). MOS tests are conducted
    under controlled conditions with a minimum number of listeners per condition.
    Results are reported as a mean with confidence intervals.
  - Word Error Rate (WER): intelligibility measured via transcription accuracy,
    computed as WER = (S + D + I) / N, where S = substitutions,
    D = deletions, I = insertions, and N = total reference words. Lower WER
    indicates higher intelligibility. WER can be obtained from human
    transcribers or automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems.

A third metric increasingly used in recent work is the Phoneme Error Rate (PER),
which measures pronunciation accuracy at the phoneme level rather than the word
level, and is especially informative for evaluating front-end G2P quality
independently of the acoustic back-end.

+-------+-----------+-----------------------------------------------+

+-------+-----------+-----------------------------------------------+
| 5     | Excellent | Completely natural, no synthetic quality      |
| 4     | Good      | Mostly natural, minor synthetic quality       |

For reference: natural human speech consistently achieves MOS 4.4–4.7; current
neural TTS systems for English reach MOS 4.2–4.5; rule-based systems typically
achieve MOS 3.0–3.8 depending on language and evaluator population (Kim et al.,
2021; Oyucu, 2023). The present Azerbaijani system achieved MOS 3.2 against a
baseline espeak-ng MOS of 2.8, representing a statistically significant
improvement of 0.4 MOS points (see Chapter III for full evaluation details).

1.1.3. Historical Development of TTS Technologies
---------------------------------------------------

The history of TTS synthesis spans nearly a century and is characterized by a
succession of distinct technological paradigms, each achieving progressively
greater naturalness at the cost of increasing data and computational requirements.

[Figure 1.2: Timeline of TTS technology development from 1939 to the present]
[Source: Tan et al. (2021). A Survey on Neural Speech Synthesis. arXiv:2106.15561.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.15561]

EARLY MECHANICAL SPEECH SYNTHESIS (1930s–1950s)
------------------------------------------------
Attempts at creating artificial speech date to the eighteenth century. In 1779,
Wolfgang von Kempelen constructed a mechanical speaking machine that simulated
the human vocal tract (Aghalari, 2023). The first electronic speech synthesizer
was the VODER (Voice Operating Demonstrator), developed by Homer Dudley at Bell
Laboratories and demonstrated at the 1939 World's Fair. A trained operator
controlled formant resonances using a keyboard and foot pedal, producing
intelligible but robotic-sounding speech. This device established a key insight:
the perceptually essential information in speech can be captured by a small
number of acoustic parameters — primarily the resonant frequencies of the vocal
tract, later formalized as formants (Tan et al., 2021).

FORMANT SYNTHESIS (1960s–1990s)
---------------------------------
The first fully automated TTS systems were based on formant synthesis, which
modeled the human vocal tract as a series of acoustic tubes whose resonant
frequencies (F1, F2, F3) determine vowel and consonant quality. The Klatt
cascade-parallel formant synthesizer (Klatt, 1980) became the dominant
architecture and formed the acoustic backbone of commercial products such as
DECtalk — famously adopted by physicist Stephen Hawking as his communication
device. Formant synthesizers produce intelligible speech but carry an
unmistakably synthetic, robotic quality (Aida-Zade & Sharifova, 2010).

In the Azerbaijani context specifically, the earliest documented Azerbaijani
TTS system was presented by Aida-Zade and Sharifova (2010) at the Third
International Conference on Problems of Cybernetics and Informatics (Baku).
Their system used a formant-concatenative hybrid approach, constructing the
acoustic basis from diphone units recorded from a donor speaker, with prosodic
characteristics applied via rules. This work represents the foundational
reference for Azerbaijani TTS research.

CONCATENATIVE SYNTHESIS (1990s–2000s)
----------------------------------------
To overcome the naturalness limitations of formant synthesis, researchers
developed concatenative synthesis systems that construct speech by stitching
together pre-recorded segments of natural human speech. Two main variants
emerged:

  Diphone synthesis: Systems such as MBROLA (Dutoit et al., 1993) use databases
  of approximately 1,000–2,000 diphone units (transitions between phoneme
  midpoints). Diphone systems achieve more natural phoneme quality than formant
  synthesis but introduce audible concatenation artifacts at unit boundaries.

  Unit selection synthesis: The Festival Speech Synthesis System (Taylor et al.,
  1998), developed at the University of Edinburgh, selects and concatenates
  larger speech units from large recorded corpora. A dynamic programming
  algorithm minimizes a combination of target cost (prosodic deviation) and join
  cost (acoustic discontinuity). Typical unit selection databases require 20–50
  hours of recorded speech.

STATISTICAL PARAMETRIC SYNTHESIS (2000s–2010s)
------------------------------------------------
Hidden Markov Model (HMM)-based statistical parametric speech synthesis (SPSS),
most fully realized in the HTS system (Zen et al., 2009), marked a significant
paradigm shift. Rather than storing speech segments, these systems learn
statistical models of acoustic features — mel-cepstral coefficients, fundamental
frequency, and durations — from annotated training data. HMM-based synthesis is
more consistent than unit selection for unseen phonetic contexts, but is
characterized by a distinctive "muffled" quality due to over-smoothing of
statistical averages (Statistical Parametric Speech Synthesis, 2015).

NEURAL TTS (2016–present)
---------------------------
The deep learning revolution produced a fundamental paradigm shift in TTS.
Google's Tacotron (Wang et al., 2017) and Tacotron 2 (Shen et al., 2018)
demonstrated that end-to-end sequence-to-sequence models could learn to map
character sequences directly to mel-spectrograms, bypassing explicit phonological
analysis. Combined with neural vocoders such as WaveNet (van den Oord et al.,
2016) or HiFi-GAN (Kong et al., 2020), these systems achieved MOS scores
indistinguishable from natural speech for English (MOS ≈ 4.5).

Subsequent architectures — FastSpeech (Ren et al., 2019), VITS (Kim et al.,
2021), and flow-matching models — further improved efficiency and quality. As of
2024, trends toward integrating large language models (LLMs) with TTS synthesis
are establishing "controllable speech synthesis" as the next standard paradigm
(arXiv:2412.06602, 2024).
