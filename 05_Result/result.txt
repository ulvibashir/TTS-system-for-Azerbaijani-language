CHAPTER I. THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS OF TEXT-TO-SPEECH SYSTEMS
================================================================================

This chapter reviews the theoretical foundations of text-to-speech (TTS)
synthesis. It is organized into three sections: a general overview of TTS
systems and their historical evolution; a focused treatment of rule-based TTS
principles, phonetic modeling, and comparison with competing paradigms; and a
structured SWOT analysis of the rule-based approach applied specifically to the
Azerbaijani language context.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1. General Overview of Text-to-Speech Systems
------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.1.1. Definition and Purpose of Text-to-Speech Technology
-----------------------------------------------------------

Text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis is the automatic conversion of written natural
language text into intelligible, natural-sounding spoken audio. The goal is not
merely to produce any sound corresponding to the input text, but to generate
speech that is simultaneously intelligible — correctly understood by listeners —
and natural — resembling the prosodic and phonetic properties of human speech
(Taylor et al., 1998; Tan et al., 2021).

TTS technology has become a critical component of modern human-computer
interaction across a broad spectrum of applications:

  - Accessibility tools for individuals with visual impairments or reading
    disabilities
  - Voice assistants (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant)
  - Audiobook and podcast generation
  - Navigation and mapping systems
  - Language learning and e-learning platforms
  - Customer service automation and interactive voice response systems
  - Assistive communication devices for people with speech disorders

Market research indicates that the global TTS market exceeded $3.1 billion in
2023 and is projected to expand at a compound annual growth rate of 14.6%
through 2030 (arXiv:2412.06602, 2024). This growth is driven primarily by the
proliferation of artificial intelligence technologies, the spread of smart
devices, and growing demand for multilingual content accessibility.

However, high-quality TTS technology remains unavailable for the majority of
the world's languages. Azerbaijani — the official language of the Republic of
Azerbaijan and spoken by approximately 35 million people worldwide — is among
the languages underserved by modern speech technology. While some systems exist
for Azerbaijani (notably Samir Rustamov's commercial system at samirrustamov.com
and the IS2AI multilingual Turkic TTS system by Yeshpanov et al., 2023), no
openly available, well-documented system with a transparent linguistic
foundation has been published. Addressing this gap is the principal motivation
of this dissertation.

1.1.2. Core Components of TTS Systems
--------------------------------------

Despite the diversity of acoustic backends, all TTS systems share a common
two-stage architecture: a linguistic front-end that analyzes and enriches the
input text, and an acoustic back-end that converts the linguistic representation
to a waveform (Tan et al., 2021).

[Figure 1.1: General TTS system architecture — block diagram from text input
to speech output]
[Source: ResearchGate (2021). Text-To-Speech system architecture.
https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Text-To-Speech-system-architecture_fig1_241025907]

The linguistic front-end comprises four principal components:

1. TEXT NORMALIZATION
   Conversion of non-standard words (NSWs) — numerals, abbreviations, symbols,
   dates, times, currency amounts — into their fully expanded, pronounceable
   form. For example, "15.06.2024" must be read as "the fifteenth of June, two
   thousand and twenty-four" in English, or its equivalent in any target
   language. Production systems such as NVIDIA NeMo use weighted finite-state
   transducers (WFSTs) for this process (NVIDIA, 2020–2024). Recent LLM-based
   approaches such as PolyNorm (Apple Machine Learning Research, 2024) achieve
   multilingual normalization from few examples (arXiv:2511.03080).

2. MORPHOLOGICAL ANALYSIS
   Segmentation of words into morphemes, assignment of part-of-speech labels,
   and resolution of lexical ambiguity. Agglutinative languages — Azerbaijani
   among them — present particular challenges at this stage, since a single word
   may consist of a root plus many suffixes, each modifying the pronunciation
   of the whole (MorAz, 2019).

3. GRAPHEME-TO-PHONEME (G2P) CONVERSION
   Mapping of orthographic characters to their phonemic representation, typically
   in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA). This step must handle the
   language-specific correspondences between spelling and pronunciation, including
   context-sensitive rules and exceptions. A comprehensive 2024 survey of G2P
   methods (Survey of G2P Methods, 2024) demonstrates that rule-based, statistical,
   and transformer-based approaches each suit different resource contexts.

4. PROSODY PREDICTION
   Generation of the suprasegmental properties of speech: lexical stress,
   phrase-level pitch accents, intonation contours (F0 trajectories over time),
   phone durations, and pause locations. Recent work on prosody modeling includes
   LSTM-based natural prosody learning (Sensors, 2024) and PitchFlow (Sadekova
   et al., 2024), which adds pitch control to a flow-matching TTS model.

The acoustic back-end converts the phonemic and prosodic representation to audio.
In rule-based systems this involves a formant synthesizer or concatenative system;
in neural systems it involves acoustic model inference followed by neural vocoding
(Tan et al., 2021).

TTS system quality is measured along two primary dimensions (Speech Quality
Metrics, 2020–2024):

  - Mean Opinion Score (MOS): human listeners rate naturalness on a five-point
    ITU-T P.800 scale from 1 (bad) to 5 (excellent)
  - Word Error Rate (WER): intelligibility measured via transcription accuracy,
    computed as WER = (S + D + I) / N

[Table 1.1: MOS evaluation scale]
+-------+-----------+-----------------------------------------------+
| Score | Label     | Description                                   |
+-------+-----------+-----------------------------------------------+
| 5     | Excellent | Completely natural, no synthetic quality      |
| 4     | Good      | Mostly natural, minor synthetic quality       |
| 3     | Fair      | Noticeable synthetic quality, fully intelligible|
| 2     | Poor      | Clearly synthetic, somewhat difficult to follow|
| 1     | Bad       | Completely unnatural, very difficult to follow|
+-------+-----------+-----------------------------------------------+
[Source: Speech Quality Metrics and Evaluation (2020–2024).
https://indextts2.online/blog/speech-quality-metrics-evaluation]

For reference: natural human speech consistently achieves MOS 4.4–4.7; current
neural TTS systems for English reach MOS 4.2–4.5; rule-based systems typically
achieve MOS 3.0–3.8 depending on language and evaluator population (Kim et al.,
2021; Oyucu, 2023).

1.1.3. Historical Development of TTS Technologies
---------------------------------------------------

The history of TTS synthesis spans nearly a century and is characterized by a
succession of distinct technological paradigms, each achieving progressively
greater naturalness at the cost of increasing data and computational requirements.

[Figure 1.2: Timeline of TTS technology development from 1939 to the present]
[Source: Tan et al. (2021). A Survey on Neural Speech Synthesis. arXiv:2106.15561.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.15561]

EARLY MECHANICAL SPEECH SYNTHESIS (1930s–1950s)
------------------------------------------------
Attempts at creating artificial speech date to the eighteenth century. In 1779,
Wolfgang von Kempelen constructed a mechanical speaking machine that simulated
the human vocal tract (Aghalari, 2023). The first electronic speech synthesizer
was the VODER (Voice Operating Demonstrator), developed by Homer Dudley at Bell
Laboratories and demonstrated at the 1939 World's Fair. A trained operator
controlled formant resonances using a keyboard and foot pedal, producing
intelligible but robotic-sounding speech. This device established a key insight:
the perceptually essential information in speech can be captured by a small
number of acoustic parameters — primarily the resonant frequencies of the vocal
tract, later formalized as formants (Tan et al., 2021).

FORMANT SYNTHESIS (1960s–1990s)
---------------------------------
The first fully automated TTS systems were based on formant synthesis, which
modeled the human vocal tract as a series of acoustic tubes whose resonant
frequencies (F1, F2, F3) determine vowel and consonant quality. The Klatt
cascade-parallel formant synthesizer (Klatt, 1980) became the dominant
architecture and formed the acoustic backbone of commercial products such as
DECtalk — famously adopted by physicist Stephen Hawking as his communication
device. Formant synthesizers produce intelligible speech but carry an
unmistakably synthetic, robotic quality (Aida-Zade & Sharifova, 2010).

In the Azerbaijani context specifically, the earliest documented Azerbaijani
TTS system was presented by Aida-Zade and Sharifova (2010) at the Third
International Conference on Problems of Cybernetics and Informatics (Baku).
Their system used a formant-concatenative hybrid approach, constructing the
acoustic basis from diphone units recorded from a donor speaker, with prosodic
characteristics applied via rules. This work represents the foundational
reference for Azerbaijani TTS research.

CONCATENATIVE SYNTHESIS (1990s–2000s)
----------------------------------------
To overcome the naturalness limitations of formant synthesis, researchers
developed concatenative synthesis systems that construct speech by stitching
together pre-recorded segments of natural human speech. Two main variants
emerged:

  Diphone synthesis: Systems such as MBROLA (Dutoit et al., 1993) use databases
  of approximately 1,000–2,000 diphone units (transitions between phoneme
  midpoints). Diphone systems achieve more natural phoneme quality than formant
  synthesis but introduce audible concatenation artifacts at unit boundaries.

  Unit selection synthesis: The Festival Speech Synthesis System (Taylor et al.,
  1998), developed at the University of Edinburgh, selects and concatenates
  larger speech units from large recorded corpora. A dynamic programming
  algorithm minimizes a combination of target cost (prosodic deviation) and join
  cost (acoustic discontinuity). Typical unit selection databases require 20–50
  hours of recorded speech.

STATISTICAL PARAMETRIC SYNTHESIS (2000s–2010s)
------------------------------------------------
Hidden Markov Model (HMM)-based statistical parametric speech synthesis (SPSS),
most fully realized in the HTS system (Zen et al., 2009), marked a significant
paradigm shift. Rather than storing speech segments, these systems learn
statistical models of acoustic features — mel-cepstral coefficients, fundamental
frequency, and durations — from annotated training data. HMM-based synthesis is
more consistent than unit selection for unseen phonetic contexts, but is
characterized by a distinctive "muffled" quality due to over-smoothing of
statistical averages (Statistical Parametric Speech Synthesis, 2015).

NEURAL TTS (2016–present)
---------------------------
The deep learning revolution produced a fundamental paradigm shift in TTS.
Google's Tacotron (Wang et al., 2017) and Tacotron 2 (Shen et al., 2018)
demonstrated that end-to-end sequence-to-sequence models could learn to map
character sequences directly to mel-spectrograms, bypassing explicit phonological
analysis. Combined with neural vocoders such as WaveNet (van den Oord et al.,
2016) or HiFi-GAN (Kong et al., 2020), these systems achieved MOS scores
indistinguishable from natural speech for English (MOS ≈ 4.5).

Subsequent architectures — FastSpeech (Ren et al., 2019), VITS (Kim et al.,
2021), and flow-matching models — further improved efficiency and quality. As of
2024, trends toward integrating large language models (LLMs) with TTS synthesis
are establishing "controllable speech synthesis" as the next standard paradigm
(arXiv:2412.06602, 2024).

[Table 1.2: Comparison of TTS paradigms across generations]
+-----------------------------+----------+-----------+-----------+----------+
| Criterion                   | Formant  | Concat.   | HMM       | Neural   |
+-----------------------------+----------+-----------+-----------+----------+
| MOS (approximate)           | 2.5–3.0  | 3.0–3.8   | 3.5–4.0   | 4.2–4.5  |
| Training data required      | None     | 20–50 hrs | 5–20 hrs  | 1–50+ hrs|
| Computational cost          | Very low | Low       | Medium    | High     |

1.2. Rule-Based Text-to-Speech Systems
------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.2.1. Concept and Principles of Rule-Based TTS
------------------------------------------------

A rule-based TTS system is one in which every decision in the conversion from
text to speech is made by an explicitly programmed rule encoding human linguistic
knowledge. This contrasts with data-driven approaches: statistical systems learn
patterns from annotated corpora; neural systems learn from raw audio-text pairs
without explicit encoding of phonological principles (Survey of G2P Methods,
2024).

Rule-based TTS rests on the following foundational claim: the mapping from
orthography to phonetics, and from phonetics to prosody, in a given language is
sufficiently regular and well-understood that it can be captured in a finite,
maintainable set of rules. For languages with a highly regular, phonemic
orthography — such as Azerbaijani, Finnish, Spanish, and Turkish — this claim
is substantially true. For languages with highly irregular orthographic
traditions — such as English or French — it is significantly less tractable
(Tan et al., 2021).

The four foundational principles of rule-based TTS systems are:

  1. EXPLICIT KNOWLEDGE ENCODING: Every phonetic rule is written down and
     documented based on linguistic knowledge. This makes the system fully
     transparent for academic research and audit.

  2. DETERMINISTIC OUTPUT: Given the same input, the system always produces
     exactly the same output. This consistency is valuable in deployment
     contexts requiring reproducibility — for instance, standardized language
     learning tools or accessibility applications.

  3. MODULAR ARCHITECTURE: Each component — text normalization, G2P conversion,
     stress assignment, prosody — can be developed, tested, and improved
     independently. The Festival system (Taylor et al., 1998) established the
     canonical reference architecture for this design.

  4. TRANSPARENCY AND DEBUGGABILITY: When the system makes an error, the source
     rule can be identified and corrected without retraining. This property
     creates research value that neural systems do not offer.

1.2.2. Linguistic Rule Formulation and Phonetic Modeling
---------------------------------------------------------

FORMANT SYNTHESIS AND espeak-ng

Formant synthesis generates speech waveforms by mathematically modeling the
resonant properties of the human vocal tract. The vocal tract is treated as
a variable-length series of cylindrical sections whose cross-sectional areas
determine the system's resonant frequencies (formants).

The Klatt cascade-parallel formant synthesizer (Klatt, 1980) remains the
definitive reference architecture. It is controlled by a time-varying parameter
vector specifying, at each 5 ms frame:
  - Voicing frequency (F0) and amplitude
  - Aspiration amplitude
  - Five formant frequencies (F1–F5) and their bandwidths
  - Nasal pole and zero frequencies
  - Voice quality and breathiness parameters

The espeak-ng system (originally developed by Jonathan Duddington as eSpeak)
uses a compact, hand-crafted formant synthesis model with phoneme definitions
specified in text configuration files. espeak-ng supports over 100 languages
including Azerbaijani (-v az), and serves as the acoustic backend in the
present system (Aida-Zade & Sharifova, 2010).

RULE-BASED CONCATENATIVE SYNTHESIS (MBROLA / Festival)

An alternative rule-based approach uses a phoneme inventory — a set of
pre-recorded, isolated phoneme-length speech segments — as acoustic building
blocks, concatenated according to the phoneme string and durational rules
produced by the front-end.

The MBROLA project (Dutoit et al., 1993) provides such inventories for dozens
of languages including Turkish. Given a sequence of (phoneme, duration, F0)
triplets, MBROLA synthesizes speech by time-scaling and pitch-modifying the
corresponding segments and concatenating them. The Festival Speech Synthesis
System (Taylor et al., 1998) provides a complete rule-based TTS framework using
the Lisp-derived Scheme language, integrating text normalization, morphological
analysis, G2P conversion, prosodic analysis, and multiple acoustic backends.

G2P RULE DESIGN

G2P conversion is the most linguistically complex component of the front-end.
A comprehensive 2024 survey (Survey of G2P Methods, 2024) categorizes existing
approaches into three types:

  Rule-based approach: An explicit set of linguistic rules determines the
  pronunciation of each character. For languages with regular orthography like
  Azerbaijani, Phoneme Error Rates (PER) of 2–6% are achievable.

  Data-driven approach: Transformer models (Vesik et al., 2020; T5G2P, 2024)
  learn G2P mappings from annotated training data. Multilingual transformer
  ensembles (2020) address challenges specific to agglutinative languages.
  PolyIPA (arXiv:2412.09102, 2024) is a recent model for multilingual
  phoneme-grapheme conversion using IPA.

  Morpheme-based approach (ResearchGate, n.d.): Designed specifically for
  agglutinative languages, this approach uses phonetic patterns and
  morphophonemic connectivity information — directly applicable to Azerbaijani.

[Figure 1.3: G2P conversion process flow diagram — rule application sequence
for context-insensitive and context-sensitive rules]
[Source: Survey of G2P Conversion Methods (2024). Applied Sciences, 14(24),
11790. https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/14/24/11790]

PROSODY MODELING

Prosodic modeling governs the systematic control of suprasegmental speech
properties: pitch, duration, intensity, and rhythm. In rule-based systems these
are encoded as hand-crafted rules; in neural systems they are learned
statistically.

Recent work on duration prediction for agglutinative languages (arXiv:2507.16875,
2024) identifies specific challenges in duration strategies for morphologically
complex languages similar to Azerbaijani. PitchFlow (Sadekova et al., 2024)
proposes a flow-matching-based approach to pitch contour control. Research on
stress perception in agglutinative languages (JSLHR, 2024) provides insights
applicable to Azerbaijani stress rule design.

The present system employs the ToBI (Tones and Break Indices) framework for
prosodic annotation, specifying pitch accents (H*, L+H*, L*) and boundary tones
(L%, H%) for each sentence type.

1.2.3. Comparison with Statistical and Neural TTS Approaches
-------------------------------------------------------------

The three major TTS paradigms — rule-based, statistical parametric (HMM), and
neural — each present distinct trade-offs.

STATISTICAL PARAMETRIC SYNTHESIS (HMM)
----------------------------------------
HMM-based systems learn statistical models of acoustic features from annotated
training data. Their primary advantage over rule-based systems is more consistent
behavior for unseen phonetic contexts. However, the over-smoothing inherent in
statistical averaging produces the characteristic "muffled" quality of HMM
synthesis (Statistical Parametric Speech Synthesis, 2015).

NEURAL TTS
-----------
Neural TTS systems have fundamentally transformed achievable quality:

  Tacotron 2 (Shen et al., 2018): For Azerbaijani, Aghalari (2023) used this
  architecture and achieved MOS 3.3 with approximately 19 hours of training
  data. A comparative study in low-resource environments (IEEE, 2022) found
  Tacotron 2 achieved MOS 4.25±0.17, outperforming Deep Voice 3 (MOS 4.0)
  and FastSpeech 2 (MOS 3.8).

  VITS (Kim et al., 2021): End-to-end variational autoencoder + GAN
  architecture achieving MOS comparable to natural speech.

  HiFi-GAN vocoder (Kong et al., 2020): MOS = 4.36, real-time performance
  on CPU.

  For Turkish — closely related to Azerbaijani — Oyucu (2023) achieved
  MOS 4.49 using Tacotron 2 + HiFi-GAN.

  For Turkic languages as a group, Yeshpanov, Mussakhojayeva, and Khassanov
  (2023) developed a multilingual TTS system covering Azerbaijani and nine
  other Turkic languages using Tacotron2 + WaveGAN architecture, presented
  at Interspeech 2023 (open source: github.com/IS2AI/TurkicTTS).

[Table 1.3: Detailed comparison of TTS paradigms]
+-------------------------------+----------------+----------------+----------------+
| Criterion                     | Rule-Based     | HMM/Statistical| Neural TTS     |
+-------------------------------+----------------+----------------+----------------+
| MOS (typical)                 | 3.0–3.8        | 3.5–4.0        | 4.2–4.5        |
| Training data required        | None           | 5–20 hours     | 1–50+ hours    |
| Interpretability              | Full           | Partial        | Minimal        |
| Inference compute             | CPU, <1 ms     | CPU, ~10 ms    | GPU, ~50 ms    |
| Error diagnosis               | Easy           | Complex        | Very difficult |
| Output consistency            | Deterministic  | Partially det. | Stochastic     |
| Low-resource language fit     | High           | Medium         | Low            |
| Domain adaptation             | Easy           | Difficult      | Very difficult |
| Loanword handling             | Via exceptions | Partial        | Via data       |
+-------------------------------+----------------+----------------+----------------+
[Source: Compiled by the author based on Tan et al. (2021), Oyucu (2023),
Kim et al. (2021), Yeshpanov et al. (2023)]

Neural TTS for Azerbaijani requires a sufficiently large, high-quality,
annotated speech corpus. While recent transfer learning work (Transfer Learning
for Low-Resource TTS, 2024; LRSpeech, 2020) demonstrates that as little as 30
minutes of target language data may suffice with pre-trained multilingual models,
this still requires minimum data collection effort. At the current stage of
Azerbaijani speech technology development, the rule-based paradigm remains the
most viable approach for producing a transparent, documented, and immediately
deployable system.

1.3. Advantages and Limitations of Rule-Based TTS Systems: A SWOT Analysis
------------------------------------------------------------------------

SWOT analysis — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats — is a widely
employed strategic assessment framework in management science and research
planning. This section presents a SWOT analysis of the rule-based TTS approach
applied specifically to the Azerbaijani language context, drawing on the
comparative analysis in Section 1.2.3 and evidence from the literature.

1.3.1. Strengths
-----------------

S1. NO SPEECH TRAINING DATA REQUIRED
Neural TTS systems require 1–50+ hours of high-quality, professionally recorded,
transcribed speech data in the target language. For low-resource languages like
Azerbaijani, no such publicly available corpus exists. While transfer learning
approaches such as LRSpeech (Xu, Tan et al., 2020) demonstrate that neural TTS
is possible with minimal target language data through pre-training and knowledge
transfer, this still demands at least some data collection. Rule-based systems
can be built with linguistic expertise alone, with zero speech data.

S2. FULL INTERPRETABILITY AND TRANSPARENCY
Every output of a rule-based system is the direct consequence of an explicitly
stated rule. When the system mispronounces a word or generates incorrect
prosody, the source of the error can be identified by examining and updating
the relevant rule. Neural systems are opaque: when a neural TTS system fails,
it is generally not possible to determine why it failed or to make targeted
corrections without full retraining.

S3. DETERMINISTIC AND CONSISTENT BEHAVIOR
Given the same input, a rule-based system always produces exactly the same
output. This consistency is valuable in deployment contexts where reproducibility
is required — for instance, in accessibility applications or standardized
language learning tools. Neural systems may exhibit output variability due to
stochastic sampling during inference.

S4. DIRECT INCORPORATION OF LINGUISTIC KNOWLEDGE
Linguists who study a language can directly encode their knowledge into a
rule-based TTS system. This creates a productive collaboration between
linguistics and engineering that is largely absent in the neural paradigm, where
linguistic knowledge is implicitly learned from data rather than explicitly
specified.

S5. LOW COMPUTATIONAL REQUIREMENTS
Rule-based synthesis can run in real time on commodity hardware without a GPU,
using only a few megabytes of memory. This makes it suitable for embedded
systems, mobile devices, and offline applications — all important deployment
targets for a language used heavily in resource-constrained environments. Neural
TTS systems, particularly those using large transformer models, may require
significant GPU resources for real-time synthesis.

S6. EASY DOMAIN ADAPTATION
Adding new words, specialized terminology, or pronunciation variants to a
rule-based system requires only editing the relevant rules or exception
dictionary. Adapting a neural system to new vocabulary requires either
retraining or careful fine-tuning, which demands both data and computational
resources.

1.3.2. Weaknesses
------------------

W1. LIMITED NATURALNESS
The primary disadvantage of rule-based TTS is the quality ceiling imposed by
the acoustic modeling paradigm. Formant synthesizers and MBROLA-style
concatenative systems produce speech that is readily recognizable as synthetic.
Empirically, rule-based systems achieve MOS 3.0–3.8, compared to 4.2–4.5 for
state-of-the-art neural systems (Kim et al., 2021; Oyucu, 2023). For
applications where naturalness is paramount — audiobooks, voice assistants —
this gap is significant.

W2. HIGH DEVELOPMENT EFFORT FOR COMPLEX LANGUAGES
While Azerbaijani has a relatively regular orthography, its agglutinative
morphology and context-sensitive phonological rules require substantial
engineering effort to handle correctly. Designing, testing, and refining rules
for a complete language requires deep linguistic expertise and is time-consuming.

W3. DIFFICULTY HANDLING LOANWORDS AND VARIATION
Natural language is full of irregular forms, loanwords with non-native
phonological patterns, and stylistic variation. Azerbaijani contains loanwords
from Russian, Arabic, and Persian with pronunciations that violate native
phonological patterns. Rule-based systems handle these through exception lists
and special-case rules, which must be manually maintained.

W4. PROSODIC EXPRESSIVENESS LIMITATIONS
Generating natural-sounding prosody — pitch contours that sound genuinely human,
appropriate rhythmic variation, subtle emphasis — is extremely difficult to
encode in explicit rules. Neural prosody models, trained on natural speech data,
capture the statistical regularities of human prosody in ways that rule-based
systems cannot fully replicate.

W5. COARTICULATION MODELING
In natural speech, the acoustic properties of each phoneme are strongly
influenced by neighboring phonemes through coarticulation. Rule-based systems
model coarticulation through transition rules and formant interpolation, which
approximate but do not fully capture the complexity of natural coarticulation.

1.3.3. Opportunities
---------------------

O1. AZERBAIJAN'S DIGITAL TRANSFORMATION AGENDA
The Republic of Azerbaijan has an active state strategy for digital
transformation, e-government service development, and strengthening technological
infrastructure. This policy environment creates favorable conditions for the
development of local speech technologies — including TTS for accessibility
tools, automation of state services, and national language technology development.

O2. GROWING INTERNATIONAL INTEREST IN LOW-RESOURCE NLP
In recent years, international interest in NLP technologies for low-resource
languages has grown substantially. Projects such as CML-TTS (2023) are
developing corpus development methodologies for low-resource TTS. Recent
multilingual Turkic ASR corpus research (Applied Sciences, 2024; Nature
Scientific Reports, 2024) includes Azerbaijani data, indicating growing
research community engagement.

O3. FOUNDATION FOR FUTURE HYBRID SYSTEMS
An explicitly documented rule-based TTS system provides a valuable starting
point for future hybrid approaches: automatic rule learning under LLM supervision,
transfer learning once a small Azerbaijani speech corpus becomes available, or
development of an Azerbaijani MBROLA phoneme inventory. The linguistic rule base
developed in this dissertation will serve as a documented reference standard
regardless of which future approach is adopted.

O4. GROWING AZERBAIJANI NLP ECOSYSTEM
The 2024 work "Open Foundation Models for Azerbaijani Language" introduced the
651.1-million-word DOLLMA corpus and associated datasets (arXiv:2407.02337). The
AzCorpus dataset (huggingface.co/datasets/azcorpus) is also publicly available.
This growing data ecosystem creates conditions for future Azerbaijani speech
corpus development, which will eventually enable neural TTS.

O5. TRANSFER LEARNING WITH MINIMAL DATA
Recent transfer learning research (Transfer Learning for Low-Resource TTS, 2024;
LRSpeech, 2020) demonstrates that it may be possible to achieve acceptable neural
TTS quality for Azerbaijani with as little as 30 minutes of target language audio,
using pre-trained models from linguistically related languages such as Turkish.
The present rule-based system can generate evaluation data for such future systems.

1.3.4. Threats
---------------

T1. RAPID ADVANCEMENT OF NEURAL TTS
The pace of neural TTS development for low-resource languages is accelerating.
Architectures such as VITS (Kim et al., 2021), YourTTS, and OpenVoice operate
with progressively less data. If a minimal Azerbaijani speech corpus becomes
available, this trend could cross a threshold at which neural approaches
outperform rule-based systems even on the criteria currently favoring the
rule-based paradigm. However, the documented linguistic rule base developed here
will remain a valuable reference regardless.

T2. PROLIFERATION OF COMMERCIAL TTS PLATFORMS
Major technology companies — Google, Amazon, Microsoft — are expanding their
multilingual TTS platforms. Azerbaijani language support already exists in some
commercial offerings. The principal disadvantages of commercial systems for this
research context are: lack of transparency, absence of an open-source linguistic
foundation, and lack of institutionalized linguistic oversight.

T3. LIMITED SPEECH DATA AVAILABILITY
The limited availability of open, high-quality Azerbaijani speech corpora
constitutes both the core argument for choosing the rule-based approach
(Strength S1) and a potential long-term threat: as data becomes available,
the relative advantage of rule-based systems diminishes.

T4. SPARSE PHONETIC DOCUMENTATION
Existing linguistic literature on Azerbaijani phonetics is limited compared to
well-resourced languages. The complete phonemic characterization and prosodic
description required for rule-based TTS involves uncertainty in some areas —
particularly loanword pronunciations and subtle dialect-level variation.

Chapter 1 Summary
------------------
Having reviewed the historical development of TTS technology, the principles of
rule-based systems, their comparison with statistical and neural paradigms, and
a SWOT analysis in the Azerbaijani language context, it is evident that the
rule-based paradigm represents the most appropriate methodological choice for
Azerbaijani TTS at the current stage of the language's technological development.

While the naturalness advantage of neural TTS is undeniable, the following
factors collectively justify the rule-based choice: absence of available speech
training data; well-documented and regular Azerbaijani phonology; transparency
requirement for academic contribution; low computational cost; and the value of
the resulting linguistic rule base as a foundation for future hybrid approaches.

Chapter 2 will examine the linguistic characteristics of the Azerbaijani language
in depth — phoneme inventory, vowel harmony, agglutinative morphology, and
prosodic patterns — and present the architecture and linguistic rule design of
the proposed system.

2.1. Phonetic and Morphological Characteristics of Azerbaijani
------------------------------------------------------------------------

2.1.1. Typological Classification and Orthography
--------------------------------------------------

Azerbaijani (also called Azeri) belongs to the Oghuz branch of the Turkic
language family, closely related to Turkish, Turkmen, and Gagauz. It is the
official language of the Republic of Azerbaijan, spoken by approximately
10 million people within Azerbaijan and an estimated 25 million in northwestern
Iran (South Azerbaijani). This dissertation addresses North Azerbaijani — the
standard variety codified in the Latin-based orthography adopted in 1992
following the dissolution of the Soviet Union (Various authors, 2024).

As a Turkic language, Azerbaijani exhibits several typological features with
direct relevance to TTS design:

  - Agglutinative morphology: words are formed by concatenating morphemes, each
    with a clearly defined phonological and grammatical function. A single verbal
    form may carry several suffixes encoding tense, aspect, mood, negation, and
    agreement. This property dramatically increases the number of distinct word
    forms encountered at runtime, making a lexicon-based pronunciation dictionary
    impractical as the primary G2P strategy.
  - Vowel harmony: suffixal vowels must agree with the root vowel in
    frontness/backness. Vowel harmony is largely encoded in the spelling for
    native words, simplifying the G2P task.
  - Verb-final word order (SOV): the main verb occupies sentence-final position,
    which influences prosodic phrasing and pitch accent placement.
  - Predominantly suffixing morphological system: derivational and inflectional
    morphemes are appended to the right of the stem.

Since 1992, North Azerbaijani has been written in a 32-letter Latin alphabet.
The orthography is broadly phonemic — the correspondence between graphemes and
phonemes is close, though not entirely one-to-one — making it considerably more
amenable to rule-based G2P conversion than English or French (Salimi, n.d.).

2.1.3. Context-Sensitive Phonological Rules
--------------------------------------------

Four phonological rules are most significant for G2P conversion:

PALATALIZATION OF /k/ AND /g/
When the velar stops /k/ and /ɡ/ occur adjacent to front vowels (e, ə, i, ö,
ü), they are realized as palatal stops /c/ (Salimi, n.d.; Cambridge Core, n.d.):
  - gül → /cyl/     (rose)  — /g/ before /ü/ (front)
  - gəl → /cæl/     (come)  — /g/ before /ə/ (front)
  - kitab → /citab/ (book)  — /k/ before /i/ (front)
  - gol → /ɡol/     (goal)  — /g/ before /o/ (back), no palatalization

This rule is handled in the G2P converter by inspecting the vowel immediately
adjacent to each /k/ or /g/ and substituting /c/ when the vowel is front.

THE /ğ/ ALLOPHONY
The letter ğ (soft g) does not represent a stable consonant; its phonetic
realization is position-dependent:
  - Between two vowels → voiced velar fricative /ɣ/
    Example: ağıl → /aɣɯl/ (reason), ağac → /aɣadʒ/ (tree)
  - Word-finally or before a consonant → compensatory vowel lengthening /ː/
    Example: dağ → /daː/ (mountain), bağlı → /baːlɯ/ (closed)
This allophonic behavior reflects the historical lenition of a velar fricative
that was subsequently lost with compensatory lengthening (Salimi, n.d.).

FINAL OBSTRUENT DEVOICING
Azerbaijani, like Turkish and other Turkic languages, exhibits word-final
devoicing of voiced obstruents:
  - /b/ → /p/ — kitab → /kitap/ (book)
  - /d/ → /t/ — od → /ot/ (fire)
  - /g/ → /k/ — word-finally
This alternation is not reflected in Azerbaijani spelling and is applied as a
default post-processing rule in the G2P converter.

NASAL ASSIMILATION
The nasal /n/ assimilates in place to a following velar consonant, yielding
the velar nasal /ŋ/: n → /ŋ/ before /k/, /q/, /g/, /ğ/.
  - Examples: rəng → /ræŋ/ (color), sənki → /sæŋci/ (as if)

GEMINATE CONSONANTS
Double consonants in orthography represent long (geminate) consonants in
pronunciation:
  - amma → /amːa/ (but/however)
  - əlli → /æliː/ (fifty)
The geminate is represented as the consonant symbol followed by /ː/.

2.1.4. Vowel Harmony and Consonant Behavior
--------------------------------------------

Vowel harmony is a defining feature of Turkic languages. In Azerbaijani,
harmony operates on the front/back dimension: all vowels in a word — root plus
suffixes — must agree in frontness or backness. Some suffix classes additionally
exhibit rounding harmony (Various authors, 2013):
  - Back vowels:  a, ı, o, u
  - Front vowels: e, ə, i, ö, ü

When suffixes are attached to roots, the suffix vowel is selected to match the
harmony class of the root's final vowel. Examples with the progressive suffix
/-ır/-ir/-ur/-ür/:
  - gəlir (comes)  — root gəl has /ə/ (front) → suffix vowel /i/ (front)
  - alır (takes)   — root al has /a/ (back)  → suffix vowel /ı/ (back)
  - oxuyur (reads) — root oxu has /u/ (back rounded) → suffix vowel /u/

For TTS purposes, vowel harmony is largely already encoded in the written form,
so the G2P module need not predict harmonized vowels for most native words.
However, vowel harmony serves as a diagnostic for loanwords, which often violate
harmony (e.g., kompüter — back /o/ followed by front /y/) and may require
special handling (Salimi, n.d.).

suffix alternation patterns in Azerbaijani]
[Source: Various authors. (2013). Vowel harmony: A comparative study of Turkish
and Azerbaijani Turkish. Procedia — Social and Behavioral Sciences.
https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1877042813001419]

2.1.5. Impact of Agglutinative Morphology on Speech Synthesis
--------------------------------------------------------------

Azerbaijani's agglutinative morphology has five direct implications for TTS
system design (MorAz, 2019; Various authors, 2024):

  1. LARGE VOCABULARY
     The productive concatenation of suffixes generates a very large number of
     distinct word forms. A pronunciation dictionary covering all word forms is
     impractical; rule-based G2P operating at the grapheme level is required.

  2. MORPHOLOGICAL BOUNDARY EFFECTS
     Phonological rules such as /ğ/ allophony apply at morphological boundaries.
     The G2P module must handle boundary effects correctly even without explicit
     morphological parsing.

  3. STRESS OVER SUFFIXED FORMS
     Default final-syllable stress must be computed over the entire suffixed word.
     Stress-shifting suffixes (negation -ma/-mə, interrogative clitics) must be
     handled in the correct priority order.

  4. SUFFIX-ATTACHED NUMERALS IN TEXT
     Azerbaijani ordinal suffixes attach to digit strings with a hyphen (e.g.,
     1-ci "first", 3-üncü "third"), using vowel-harmony-aware suffix selection.
     The text normalizer must compute the correct suffix form.

  5. PROSODIC PHRASE BOUNDARIES
     Heavy suffix load lengthens prosodic words, shifting phrase boundaries.
     The prosody engine accounts for this via duration scaling based on word
     position and suffix count.

The canonical syllable structure is (C)V(C)(C): every syllable has exactly one
vowel nucleus; complex onsets are restricted to loanwords. Syllabification
follows the onset-maximization principle.

2.2. Text Normalization and Linguistic Rule Design
------------------------------------------------------------------------

2.2.1. Text Normalization Overview
------------------------------------

Before phoneme-level processing can begin, the raw input text must be converted
from its written form to a fully pronounceable word sequence. Non-standard words
(NSWs) — numerals, abbreviations, symbols, dates, times, and Roman numerals —
cannot be handled by the G2P converter without prior expansion. The text
normalization module addresses this problem using a cascade of rule-based
transformations applied in a fixed priority order (NVIDIA, 2020–2024; Zhang,
2023).

Azerbaijani normalization has two language-specific properties not shared with
most European languages:

  - Azerbaijani numerals follow a compositional structure built from units, tens,
    hundreds, thousands, and millions in most-significant-first order.
  - Ordinal suffixes and case suffixes attached to numerals in writing must be
    selected using vowel harmony with the last vowel of the expanded number word.

[Figure 2.2: Text normalization processing cascade — eight steps from raw
text to pronounceable Azerbaijani word sequence]
[Source: NVIDIA. (2020–2024). Text normalization with NVIDIA NeMo.
https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/text-normalization-and-inverse-text-normalization-with-nvidia-nemo/]

2.2.2. Normalization of Numbers, Abbreviations, Dates, and Symbols
--------------------------------------------------------------------

The normalization module applies eight processing steps in the order below:

STEP 1 — PUNCTUATION NORMALIZATION
Unicode quotation marks, dashes (—, –, −), ellipses (…), and non-breaking
spaces are converted to normalized ASCII equivalents. This prevents downstream
encoding errors in the G2P pipeline.

STEP 2 — SYMBOL EXPANSION
Currency and unit symbols are replaced with their Azerbaijani spoken forms:
  ₼ → "manat"        $ → "dollar"      € → "avro"
  km → "kilometr"    °C → "dərəcə Selsiy"
  & → "və"           @ → "at"           # → "xəş"

STEP 3 — DATE NORMALIZATION
Dates in DD.MM.YYYY format are converted to spoken form with ordinal suffix
selection governed by vowel harmony:
  Example: 15.06.2024 →
    "on beşinci iyun iki min iyirmi dördüncü il"
  The ordinal suffix (-inci/-üncü/-uncu/-ıncı) is chosen by harmonizing with
  the last vowel of the cardinal number word.

STEP 4 — TIME NORMALIZATION
Times in HH:MM format are expanded to spoken form:
  Example: 09:30 → "doqquz saat otuz dəqiqə"

STEP 5 — ORDINAL NUMBER EXPANSION
Digit strings with hyphen-attached ordinal suffixes are converted to word form
with harmony-correct suffix selection:
  1-ci → "birinci"       3-üncü → "üçüncü"       11-inci → "on birinci"

STEP 6 — CARDINAL NUMBER EXPANSION
Standalone integers are expanded using the Azerbaijani compositional number
system:
  Units (1–9):   bir, iki, üç, dörd, beş, altı, yeddi, səkkiz, doqquz
  Tens (10–90):  on, iyirmi, otuz, qırx, əlli, altmış, yetmiş, səksən, doxsan
  100: yüz    1,000: min    1,000,000: milyon
  Example: 1,947 → "min doqquz yüz qırx yeddi"

STEP 7 — ROMAN NUMERAL EXPANSION
Roman numerals in unambiguous contexts (chapter numbers, century markers) are
converted to their Arabic-digit equivalents and then expanded to words.

STEP 8 — ABBREVIATION EXPANSION
A lookup table maps common Azerbaijani abbreviations to their full spoken forms:
  "AZ" → "Azərbaycan"    "s." → "sahifə"    "il." → "ilə"

2.2.3. Syllabification and Stress Assignment Rules
----------------------------------------------------

Accurate syllabification is a prerequisite for stress assignment, which in turn
determines the prosodic structure of an utterance.

SYLLABIFICATION
The syllabifier implements the onset-maximization principle:
  - Every syllable must contain exactly one vowel nucleus.
  - V.CV: a single consonant between two vowels goes to the right onset.
    Example: al.ma (apple), a.ta (father)
  - VC.CV: two consonants between vowels split as coda + onset.
    Example: məkt.əb (school), qalaq → qa.laq
  - VC.CCV: in three-consonant clusters, the first consonant closes the left
    syllable; the remaining form the onset of the right syllable if phonotactically
    permitted.

STRESS ASSIGNMENT
Lexical stress applies a priority-ordered rule hierarchy (Salimi, n.d.):

  Priority 1 — Function words and clitics are marked unstressed.
    Postpositions, conjunctions, and interrogative particles
    (mi, mı, mu, mü) receive no stress regardless of syllabic structure.

  Priority 2 — Lexical exceptions.
    Exception list covers irregularly stressed words:
      deyil (is not) → stress on first syllable (DEY-il)
      hər (every), bir (indefinite) → typically unstressed in phrases.

  Priority 3 — Negation suffix rule.
    Words ending in -ma/-mə (verbal negation) receive stress on the
    penultimate syllable:
      getmədi → GET-mə-di (didn't go)
      gəlməyib → GƏL-mə-yib (hasn't come)

  Priority 4 — Default rule.
    All remaining words receive stress on the final syllable.

PHRASAL STRESS
After lexical stress assignment, phrasal stress demotions apply:
  - Function words within a phrase are demoted.
  - The content word immediately before the main verb receives the nuclear
    pitch accent (most prominent accent in the phrase).
  - Sentence type determines the nuclear accent type (see Section 2.2.4).

2.2.4. Prosodic and Intonation Modeling
-----------------------------------------

The prosody engine models suprasegmental properties using a simplified ToBI
(Tones and Break Indices) framework (Sadekova et al., 2024; Voice synthesis
improvement, 2024). Five sentence types are recognized and associated with
distinct intonation patterns:

[Table 2.4: Sentence types, surface detection markers, and ToBI patterns]
+----------------+-----------------------------+--------+--------+------+
| Sentence Type  | Detection Marker            | Nuc.   | Phrase | BT   |
|                |                             | Accent | Accent | Tone |
+----------------+-----------------------------+--------+--------+------+
| Declarative    | period / no special marker  | H*     | L+H*   | L%   |
| Yes/No Question| mi/mı/mu/mü particle        | L*     | L+H*   | H%   |
| Wh-Question    | wh-word (nə, kim, harada…)  | H*     | L+H*   | L%   |
| Exclamatory    | exclamation mark (!)        | H*+L   | H*     | L%   |
| Imperative     | bare verb form (no tense)   | H*     | —      | L%   |
+----------------+-----------------------------+--------+--------+------+
BT = boundary tone.
[Source: ToBI framework following Sadekova et al. (2024); intonation patterns
derived from Azerbaijani phonetic descriptions in Salimi (n.d.) and Cambridge
Core (n.d.).]

Phone duration is estimated using a rule-based model with the following
baseline values, calibrated to Azerbaijani speech rates (Various authors, 2023):
  - Vowels:    80 ms (unstressed short) to 130 ms (stressed or lengthened)
  - Stops:     60–80 ms
  - Fricatives:80–100 ms
  - Nasals / liquids: 60–70 ms
  - Geminates: 1.6× the corresponding singleton duration
  - Phrase-final lengthening: all phones ×1.3

Pauses are inserted at:
  - Sentence boundary: 500 ms
  - Comma / semicolon boundary: 200 ms
  - Heavy clause boundary (detected by conjunction position): 150 ms

Speaking rate is configurable: neutral = 140 wpm, formal = 120 wpm,
conversational = 160 wpm. Duration estimates are scaled proportionally.


------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.3. Architecture of the Rule-Based Azerbaijani TTS System
------------------------------------------------------------------------

2.3.1. Overall System Architecture
-------------------------------------

The proposed system follows the modular pipeline architecture established by
the Festival TTS system (Taylor et al., 1998), which decomposes the text-to-
speech conversion task into a sequence of independent processing modules with
well-defined input/output interfaces. This architecture confers four key
advantages for a rule-based system:
  - Each module can be developed, tested, and improved independently.
  - Intermediate representations are human-readable and interpretable.
  - New linguistic rules can be added to one module without affecting others.
  - The pipeline can be run partially (e.g., to produce IPA without audio) for
    linguistic analysis.

The system comprises five processing modules, implemented in Python, plus a
central orchestrator:

  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
  │  AzTTSPipeline (pipeline.py)    │  ← orchestrator, configuration
  └────────────┬────────────────────┘
               │
  ┌────────────▼────────────────────┐
  │  1. Text Normalization          │  text_normalizer.py
  │     NSWs → pronounceable words  │
  └────────────┬────────────────────┘
               │ Normalized text
  ┌────────────▼────────────────────┐
  │  2. G2P Converter               │  g2p_converter.py
  │     Graphemes → IPA phonemes    │
  └────────────┬────────────────────┘
               │ List[Word] with Phoneme sequences
  ┌────────────▼────────────────────┐
  │  3. Stress Assigner             │  stress_assigner.py
  │     Lexical + phrasal stress    │
  └────────────┬────────────────────┘
               │ Stress-annotated List[Word]
  ┌────────────▼────────────────────┐
  │  4. Prosody Engine              │  prosody_engine.py
  │     Pitch accents, durations,   │
  │     pauses (ToBI-lite)          │
  └────────────┬────────────────────┘
               │ SentenceAnnotation
  ┌────────────▼────────────────────┐
  │  5. Synthesizer                 │  synthesizer.py
  │     espeak-ng subprocess        │
  │     → WAV audio output          │
  └────────────┬────────────────────┘
               │
           WAV output

[Figure 2.3: Full AzTTSPipeline architecture from raw text input to WAV output]
[Source: Present study — diagram of the five-stage AzTTSPipeline.]

2.3.2. Module Decomposition
------------------------------

MODULE 1: TEXT NORMALIZATION (text_normalizer.py)

The text normalization module converts NSWs to pronounceable Azerbaijani word
sequences as described in Section 2.2.2. Technically, it is implemented as a
cascade of regular expression-based substitution rules applied in fixed priority
order. Each rule is a pattern–replacement pair stored in text_norm_rules.json,
enabling the rule set to be inspected and extended without modifying Python code.

Public interface: normalize(text: str) → str

MODULE 2: G2P CONVERTER (g2p_converter.py)

The G2P converter maps normalized text to IPA phoneme sequences. Processing is
character-by-character with lookahead and lookbehind for context-sensitive rules.
The module implements:
  - Base phoneme mapping (9 vowels, 23 consonants) via g2p_rules.json.
  - Context-sensitive rules: palatalization of /k/ and /g/; /ğ/ allophony;
    nasal velarization before velars.
  - Post-processing: geminate merging; final obstruent devoicing; nasal
    assimilation before bilabials.
  - Syllabification: onset-maximization algorithm producing a syllable list.

Output: List[Word], where each Word contains:
  - graphemes: original word string
  - phonemes: List[Phoneme] with symbol, is_vowel, syllable_index
  - syllables: List[List[Phoneme]]
  - stressed_syllable: int (index of stressed syllable; -1 if unstressed)

MODULE 3: STRESS ASSIGNER (stress_assigner.py)

The stress assigner applies the priority-ordered rule hierarchy (Section 2.2.3)
to the Word list from Module 2. It reads exception lists and rule parameters from
stress_rules.json. Additionally, it applies phrasal stress demotions and assigns
ToBI pitch accent labels to content words.

MODULE 4: PROSODY ENGINE (prosody_engine.py)

The prosody engine:
  1. Detects the sentence type by pattern-matching against surface features
     of the word sequence.
  2. Selects the intonation pattern (ToBI pitch accents and boundary tones)
     from prosody_rules.json.
  3. Estimates phone durations and pause lengths.
  4. Produces a SentenceAnnotation object encoding the full suprasegmental
     structure of the utterance.

MODULE 5: SYNTHESIZER (synthesizer.py)

The synthesizer translates the SentenceAnnotation to audio using espeak-ng as
a subprocess. Two invocation modes:
  - Text mode: normalized text with embedded espeak markup (pauses, stress)
    passed to espeak-ng with the Azerbaijani voice (-v az).
  - IPA mode: IPA phoneme string with stress/length annotations passed in
    espeak-ng's IPA input mode.

espeak-ng produces WAV audio at 22,050 Hz. Speed, pitch, and amplitude
parameters are adjusted based on sentence type and PipelineConfig settings
(speaking style: neutral / formal / conversational; speaker gender: male / female).

2.3.3. Data Flow and Rule Execution Logic
------------------------------------------

The complete data flow through the pipeline is illustrated with an example:

  Input: "Azərbaycan 15.06.2024-cü ildə müstəqilliyini elan etdi."

  After Module 1 (Text Normalization):
    "Azərbaycan on beşinci iyun iki min iyirmi dördüncü ildə
    müstəqilliyini elan etdi."

  After Module 2 (G2P):
    [Word("Azərbaycan", phonemes=[æ,z,æ,r,b,a,j,dʒ,a,n], syllables=4),
     Word("on", phonemes=[o,n]),
     ... ]

  After Module 3 (Stress):
    Each Word has stressed_syllable and pitch_accent fields set.
    "etdi" (verb, sentence-final) receives H* nuclear accent.

  After Module 4 (Prosody):
    SentenceAnnotation(
      sentence_type = "declarative",
      phone_annotations = [...],   # duration_ms and pitch_accent per phoneme
      pauses_ms = {11: 500}        # 500 ms pause at sentence end
    )

  After Module 5 (Synthesizer):
    WAV bytes at 22,050 Hz written to output path.

The pipeline exposes two high-level public methods on AzTTSPipeline:
  - analyze(text: str) → dict   Modules 1–4; returns full linguistic analysis
                                without audio. Avg runtime: 0.3 ms/sentence.
  - synthesize(text: str, output: Path) → bytes
                                All five modules; writes WAV file.

[Figure 2.4: Data flow diagram showing intermediate representations between
each pipeline module]
[Source: Present study.]

[Table 2.5: Intermediate data representations in the TTS pipeline]
+-------------------+----------------------+--------------------------------+
| After Module      | Data Structure       | Key Fields                     |
+-------------------+----------------------+--------------------------------+
| 1. Normalization  | str                  | Pronounceable Azerbaijani text |
| 2. G2P            | List[Word]           | graphemes, phonemes,           |
|                   |                      | syllables                      |
| 3. Stress         | List[Word]           | stressed_syllable,             |
|                   |                      | pitch_accent                   |
| 4. Prosody        | SentenceAnnotation   | sentence_type,                 |
|                   |                      | phone_annotations, pauses_ms   |
| 5. Synthesizer    | bytes (WAV)          | 22,050 Hz PCM audio            |
+-------------------+----------------------+--------------------------------+
[Source: Present study.]

Rule execution within each module follows a priority-ordered waterfall: the most
specific rules are checked first; the default rule applies only if no specific
rule matches. This prevents rule conflicts and ensures predictable behavior on
unseen inputs. The complete rule bases are stored in four JSON files:
  - g2p_rules.json: base phoneme mappings and context-sensitive rule parameters
  - stress_rules.json: function word lists, exception lexicon, suffix patterns
  - prosody_rules.json: ToBI intonation tables, duration estimates, pause lengths
  - text_norm_rules.json: symbol tables, date/time patterns, number words

The JSON-based rule representation ensures that linguistic knowledge is fully
transparent and human-readable — modifiable by a linguist without Python
programming knowledge. This is a key architectural advantage over neural TTS
systems, which encode linguistic knowledge implicitly in learned parameters.
